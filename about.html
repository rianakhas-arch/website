My Evolving Approach to Engineering Design

My approach to engineering design has evolved over the course of this semester. Initially, I viewed design similarly to my marathon training: as a paced, iterative process where progress comes from consistency, reflection, and sustained effort. I believed that carefully framing problems and embracing uncertainty would naturally lead to stronger outcomes.

While this perspective remains valuable, I came to realize that it was incomplete. I began to recognize that engineering design is not only iterative, but also messy, constrained, and often nonlinear. Rather than becoming more stable through careful pacing, the design process continuously evolves and often requires revisiting earlier decisions. This led me to view engineering design as a process that must remain adaptable, where problem framing is not a fixed early step but something that develops alongside the design.

This shift in understanding is reflected in my work during the Praxis II accessible filming project. Although my team invested significant time in framing the problem, feedback from our Beta presentation led us to substantially reframe the opportunity based on user needs and practical constraints. This demonstrated that even well-considered initial decisions must remain flexible, reinforcing the importance of adaptability in my design approach.

Through these experiences, I developed a deeper understanding of the non-linear nature of engineering design. Stakeholder needs, physical constraints, and usability considerations often required revisiting earlier assumptions rather than progressing in a clean sequence. This challenged my initial belief that pacing alone is sufficient, and highlighted the need to actively respond to changing conditions throughout the design process.

Additionally, I have come to see iteration not as repetition, but as a process of learning through testing and failure. Early prototypes often revealed issues that were not apparent during planning, reinforcing the importance of building and testing early rather than waiting for a fully resolved plan.

As a result, my current position is that engineering design is a cyclical and evolving process that requires both reflection and action. Compared to my initial perspective, which emphasized pacing and reflection as the primary drivers of progress, I now place greater emphasis on adaptability, decision-making under constraints, and using uncertainty as a driver for iteration.

Current context

Currently, I am a first-year engineering science student developing my design practice through team-based projects. I recognize that my understanding of engineering design is still fresh and evolving and I understand and acknowledge my constraints in the field. But, I'm excited to continue learning and developing my design practice through future projects and experiences.

My values

My design practice is guided by several key principles:

  • Honesty: I value collaboration rooted in honesty and open communication, where ideas can be shared freely and constructively.
  • Accountability: I take responsibility for my work, acknowledge mistakes, and continuously learn from them while holding myself to a high standard.
  • Kindness: I strive to create an inclusive environment where everyone feels respected, supported, and comfortable contributing their ideas.
