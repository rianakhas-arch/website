My Evolving Approach to Engineering Design

My approach to engineering design has evolved over the course of this semester. Initially, I viewed design similarly to my marathon training: as a paced, iterative process where progress comes from consistency, reflection, and sustained effort. I believed that taking time to carefully frame problems and embracing uncertainty would naturally lead to stronger outcomes.

While this perspective remains valuable, I came to realize that it was incomplete. I began to recognize that engineering design is not only iterative, but also messy, constrained, and often nonlinear. Even when effort and consistency are applied, the process does not always unfold as planned. For example, although my team invested significant time in framing the problem, feedback from our Beta presentation led us to substantially reframe the opportunity. This marked a shift in my understanding of engineering design: rather than a process that becomes more stable through careful pacing, I began to see it as one that continuously evolves and often requires revisiting earlier decisions. This demonstrated that problem framing is not a fixed early step, but something that continues to evolve alongside the design.

Through this experience, I came to better understand the inherently non-linear nature of engineering design. Stakeholder needs, physical constraints, and usability considerations required us to revisit earlier assumptions multiple times, rather than progressing in a clean sequence. This challenged my earlier belief that pacing alone is sufficient.

I now recognize that although effective design does require embracing uncertainty, it is more than that, it requires active decision-making under uncertainty. Trade-offs must often be made before complete clarity is achieved. In our project, considerations such as stability, adjustability, and ease of use frequently conflicted, requiring us to prioritize certain outcomes rather than attempting to optimize all aspects simultaneously.

Additionally, I have come to see iteration not as simple repetition, but as a process of learning through testing and failure. Early prototypes revealed issues that were not apparent during planning. For instance, our initial concept involved mounting a camera at the back of the wheelchair and controlling it through a flexible gooseneck. However, prototyping revealed that the user's range of motion was insufficient to effectively operate this system. This reinforced the importance of building and testing early, rather than waiting for a fully resolved plan.

As a result, my current position is that engineering design is a cyclical and evolving process that requires both reflection and action. Compared to my initial position, which emphasized pacing and reflection as the primary drivers of progress, I now place greater emphasis on adaptability, decision-making under constraints, and using uncertainty as a driver for iteration.

Current context

Currently, I am a first-year engineering science student developing my design practice through team-based projects. I recognize that my understanding of engineering design is still fresh and evolving and I understand and acknowledge my constraints in the field. But, I'm excited to continue learning and developing my design practice through future projects and experiences.

My values

My design practice is guided by several key principles:

  • Honesty: I value collaboration rooted in honesty and open communication, where ideas can be shared freely and constructively.
  • Accountability: I take responsibility for my work, acknowledge mistakes, and continuously learn from them while holding myself to a high standard.
  • Kindness: I strive to create an inclusive environment where everyone feels respected, supported, and comfortable contributing their ideas.
