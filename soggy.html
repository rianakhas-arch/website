Praxis I Design Project

Soggy Sandwiches

This project addressed the problem of sandwiches becoming soggy during storage due to moisture transfer. Our team developed and tested multiple concepts to improve freshness and usability, ultimately recommending a pull-out layered container system.

Quick Facts

  • Course: ESC101
  • Focus: Food freshness and convenience
  • Final concept: Pull-out layered container
  • Core issue: Moisture transfer into bread
Back to Projects Go to CTMFs
One-Pager

Opportunity

Problem and Stakeholders

As commuter students who regularly pack lunches in advance, we identified a common issue: sandwiches often become soggy by the time they are consumed. Over several hours, moisture from ingredients such as vegetables or condiments migrates into the bread, degrading texture and overall eating quality. This problem reduces user satisfaction despite otherwise convenient meal preparation. As a result, we identified an opportunity to design a solution that minimizes moisture transfer and preserves sandwich quality until consumption.

Design Process

How the concept was developed

We explored five concepts, including Pull-Out Layers, Skewer, Salty Sponges, Fan, and Parchment Paper separation. Each concept was evaluated using methods such as assembly time, moisture absorption, and spillage testing, then compared through tools like pairwise comparison and Pugh charts.

Final Design

Recommended concept

The final concept was a pull-out layered container that keeps wet ingredients separated from the bread until consumption. This approach directly targeted the main source of sogginess while remaining practical and reasonably easy to use.

Results

Evidence from testing

  • Assembly time was about 53 seconds.
  • Bread compression remained low compared with alternatives.
  • The concept performed best overall in final selection.

Reflection

What I learned

This project showed me that strong design decisions come from balancing user needs with technical evaluation. Several concepts worked in one area, but the final recommendation had to combine effectiveness, convenience, and practicality. It was an early example of how structured testing can support better design reasoning.

Connection to my position

How this reflects my design approach

This project reflects my tendency to approach design through evidence-based comparison and user-focused problem framing. Even in an early design project, I was already drawn to solutions that were not only functional, but also realistic for everyday use.
